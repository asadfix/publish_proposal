A paper from my MSc dissertation

How much of public sentiment is really the public, and how much is just the platform?

Lots of studies show sentiment differs across Twitter, Reddit, YouTube and Quora, then stop. My contribution is to treat each platform as a measuring instrument, so I can separate how much of that difference is the platform itself from how much is real disagreement, and put the platforms on one comparable scale. As far as I can find, no one has done this for machine-labelled sentiment.

Why none of these is novel enough to carry a paper on its own
Each one applies an established method to a new dataset. Solid work, and the integrated four-platform UK dataset has real value, but none is a research-level novelty on its own:
  • Multi-platform sentiment has been done. Pellert et al. 2022 validated text sentiment across platforms against self-reports, and the Measuring Hate Speech corpus (Kennedy et al. 2020, preprint; peer-reviewed as Sachdeva et al. 2022) spans YouTube, Twitter and Reddit. The new part here is the UK economic-policy domain, not the method.
  • Transformers beating older models has been settled since Devlin et al. 2019, and confirmed for policy sentiment elsewhere (for example Souza et al. 2024). Showing it again on a new dataset confirms a known result rather than extending it.
  • Forecasting opinion from social media goes back to O'Connor et al. 2010, who tied tweet sentiment to consumer-confidence series (more reliably than to candidate-preference polls); Prophet (Taylor & Letham 2018) is just the tool. Short-term predictability of sentiment is not itself a new claim.
That is exactly why the section below exists. The dataset and domain are valuable, but the publishable novelty is the measurement reframing, not the pipeline, the classifier, or the forecast.

What is not yet there: a rigorous same-event, platform-by-platform comparison that isolates measurement differences from topic composition and who-posts effects. That is the gap the paper fills.

What we add before publishing

My contribution the novelty

Plenty of studies show sentiment differs across platforms, then stop there. I treat each platform as a measuring instrument, the way a survey researcher treats phone, online and face to face as three ways of asking the same question. That one shift turns a loose observation into something you can measure, test, and correct.

Survey research (known)

Survey mode

Phone, online, face to face. The same person answers differently depending on how you ask.

→
My version (new)

Social platform

Twitter, Reddit, YouTube, Quora. The same opinion can surface differently on each one.

On top of the dissertation above, four concrete moves turn it into a publishable paper. As far as I can find, they have not been brought together for machine-labelled sentiment across platforms before:

01the reframe

Platform as measuring instrument

Treat each platform as the instrument doing the measuring, the way a survey treats phone, web and face to face. This turns "platforms differ" from a finding you report into a measurement problem you can test.

02main result

Variance split: platform vs. real disagreement

A generalizability-theory decomposition that puts a number on how much of the cross-platform gap is the platform itself and how much is genuine disagreement, using NRC emotion components as indicators and a re-checked human sample as the independent yardstick.

03reusable

Invariance and DIF testing

Measurement invariance and differential item functioning, run on several emotion signals per event, to say which platform comparisons are sound before you trust them.

04first step

Test equating onto one scale

Use shared policy events as anchor items to line the platforms onto one comparable scale. Where no stable anchor exists, the honest answer is that they cannot be fairly compared, which is itself worth reporting.

How this is unique

Each piece exists on its own. What I cannot find anywhere is the combination: measurement-equivalence tools, applied to machine-labelled sentiment, compared across two or more platforms, with the platform itself as the thing being tested. The nearest work each stops one step short, survey self-report rather than model-labelled text (Boyd et al. 2024), annotators rather than platforms (Sachdeva et al. 2022), or correlation rather than equating (Pellert et al. 2022).

To be clear about what is mine: the split between how it is measured and who posts is not mine to claim. Survey work drew it first (Sen et al., Schober et al.). The closest adjacent use of the toolkit is Cernat et al. 2025, who run a multitrait-multimethod model treating survey and digital-trace data as different methods, though not across social platforms and not on sentiment. What is new is bringing the full toolkit onto the platform itself, for machine-labelled sentiment across platforms. I am not claiming to recover a "true" opinion, only a comparable one.

What the contribution delivers
New knowledge
It turns a question that is normally assumed away into a measured quantity: how much of a cross-platform sentiment gap is real opinion, and how much is a recording artefact of the platform.
In practice

My dissertation measured taxation at about 65% negative, with Twitter spiking sharper than YouTube on the same announcements. Is that a real split in opinion, or just Twitter's format amplifying anger? This puts a number on it, where Pellert et al. (2022) validated cross-platform sentiment against self-reports but never separated the two.

How others build on it
A drop-in diagnostic. Any team comparing sentiment across platforms can run the invariance check to see whether their comparison holds, and equate the scales where it does. It turns a one-off finding into a reusable instrument.
In practice

A researcher comparing welfare-reform sentiment across Reddit and YouTube could run the same check before pooling them into one trend, rather than assuming the scales match. That is the reusable measurement step Schober et al. (2016) argued social media analysis needs.

What it advances
It gives the "social media as a barometer of opinion" claim a falsifiable measurement footing, advancing computational social science, public-opinion measurement, and the validity toolkit for applied NLP.
In practice

Before a cross-platform sentiment index is reported as public opinion, this says which platforms are safe to combine and which are not. It is the kind of total-error scrutiny Sen et al. (2021) brought to digital traces, now carried to machine-labelled sentiment.

Where this could lead

The same diagnostic, in the hands of the organisations that already watch social sentiment, and what it lets them build.

A Budget mood index a department can report

Government · HM Treasury, DWP
Today

The Treasury reads Budget reaction off X, while Reddit and YouTube tell a different story and cannot be pooled into one figure, so a single platform ends up standing in for the public.

With this method

Test whether the platforms are measuring the same reaction to the same announcement, then combine only the ones that are, and flag the ones that are not.

Becomes

A defensible public-mood index, updated around each fiscal event, that an official could brief on without a methodologist objecting that the platforms were never comparable. The proactive tool my dissertation set out to build, now on a measurement footing.

Industry · bank, retailer, social-listening firm
Today

A bank or retailer tracking reaction to a price change sums TikTok, X and Reddit into one brand-sentiment number that quietly mixes each platform's house style with real opinion.

Why the platform part is the hard part

Two kinds of bias, not one

There are two reasons social media can mislead you. The first is well studied. The second, the one I work on, is mostly ignored for sentiment pulled from text, and you cannot fix it the same way.

Already studied

Who posts

The wrong people post, or the slice you collect is skewed, so the crowd is not representative. You fix it by re-weighting to match the population.

How it is measured

Observations suggest that even when the opinion is the same, each platform records it differently. Re-weighting cannot fix that. The method below is designed to test whether this is true, then measure and ideally correct it.

Why this fits ICWSM

Where it sits in the conference

ICWSM has a long line of work asking whether social media data faithfully measures public opinion. This paper is a direct continuation of it, moving the question from "is the sample representative" to "is the measurement equivalent."

Tufekci (2014) set out the validity and representativeness problems of social media big data at ICWSM, including the field's over-reliance on a single platform. Morstatter et al. (2013) asked whether the data you pull even represents the platform it comes from. Cohen and Ruths (2013) showed that attitude inference from social media had been systematically over-reported because validation samples were skewed. O'Connor et al. (2010) linked text sentiment to opinion series, but treated platform noise as something that averages out.

These are about who is in the data, how it is sampled, and whether inferred labels can be trusted. None asks whether the same opinion is recorded equivalently across platforms once a model has labelled it. That measurement-equivalence question is what I add, with psychometric tools the community has not yet applied to machine-labelled cross-platform sentiment. That is the fit: the same long-standing ICWSM concern, carried to the next unexamined source of error.

Honest limits

What this does not do

I would rather be upfront about the edges. These are the things a careful reviewer will ask about.
  • Sentiment is not the same as support. I measure the mood in the text, which stands in for opinion. I keep that distinction clear.
  • It is observational, and this is the biggest caveat. The same event draws different people and different sub-topics on each platform, so what I put down to the platform is an upper bound until I can follow the same users across platforms. I keep that front and centre.
  • It is these four platforms. The findings are about them, not every platform.
  • The fix needs a real test. Lining the platforms up is step one. Checking whether mode-adjusted sentiment tracks a UK opinion series (British Social Attitudes, British Election Study, or YouGov economic trackers) would come later, but the contribution does not depend on it.

"To my knowledge, I am the first to treat each platform as a measurement mode and apply the psychometric equivalence-and-equating toolkit to machine-classified policy sentiment, testing whether platform differences reflect genuine attitudinal divergence or mode-induced measurement non-equivalence, and reporting where cross-platform comparison is and is not justified."
